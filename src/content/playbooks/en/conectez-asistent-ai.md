steps:
  - "Only use <strong>connectors from the assistant's official list</strong>, made by that company or by a maker you know. A connector is a small extension that links the AI assistant to other apps (the technology behind it is called MCP, Model Context Protocol) — and, like any extension, it can also be made by people with bad intentions."
  - "<strong>Connect one thing at a time, only what you use.</strong> Don't link email, files and calendar \"just in case\" — every extra link means more data the assistant can reach."
  - "Think about <strong>what is in the account you are linking</strong>: your email holds password resets, contracts, invoices. If there is sensitive data or client data in there, don't connect it — or use a separate account, just for this."
  - "Keep <strong>manual approval</strong> turned on (\"ask me before sending or deleting anything\"). A connected assistant also reads messages and documents received from other people — and some can contain hidden instructions that trick it. Your confirmation is the safety net; see also the playbook about the assistant doing things on your behalf."
  - "<strong>Tidy up regularly:</strong> go to myaccount.google.com/connections (Google) or to your Microsoft account settings under \"Apps and services\" and remove the connectors and apps you no longer use."
donts:
  - "Don't install connectors found in ads, on forums or received in messages — only use the official list inside the assistant's app."
  - "Don't connect your company email or files with client data without your IT team's agreement."
  - "Don't type your account password directly into a connector — a genuine connection goes through the official Google/Microsoft page, which shows you exactly what access you are giving."
  - "Don't leave apps connected that you no longer use — forgotten access means forgotten risk."
recognize:
  - "<strong>It asks for more than it promises:</strong> a connector \"just for reading your calendar\" that also wants to send emails in your name."
  - "A maker you can't find anywhere: no website, no contact details, appeared yesterday."
  - "It's promoted aggressively — \"the connector that does everything for you\", insistent ads, private messages recommending it."
  - "<strong>After connecting, the assistant does unexpected things</strong> — it sends, shares or searches for things you didn't ask for. Remove the connector immediately."

**First, breathe.** Connecting things isn't bad in itself — that is exactly what makes an assistant truly useful. Think of connectors as *the keys to your house*: you give them only to someone you trust, only for the room they need, and you can take them back at any time from your account settings.
